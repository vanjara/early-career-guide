# Offer & Negotiation

## The most common mistake

Accepting on the spot.

A recruiter calls with an offer. You're excited, relieved, maybe desperate. They ask if you can confirm today. You say yes.

You just left money on the table and skipped your only chance to negotiate.

Companies expect negotiation. Recruiters are not offended by it. The offer is not withdrawn because you asked. In ten years of working you will tell this story — make sure it ends well.

---

## When you get the call

You do not need to respond immediately. Ever.

> *"Thank you so much — I'm really excited about this. Can I have a few days to review everything carefully?"*

Standard ask is 3-5 business days. Most companies give it without question. If they pressure you for same-day, that's a signal about the company culture.

Use those days to:
- Research market rate for the role
- Evaluate the full package, not just base salary
- Decide what you actually want to ask for

---

## Research market rate first

Never negotiate without data. Feelings don't move offers. Numbers do.

| Source | Best for |
|---|---|
| **Levels.fyi** | Tech roles — most accurate, self-reported with verification |
| **Glassdoor** | Broad — directionally useful, less precise |
| **Wall Street Oasis** | Finance and consulting compensation |
| **LinkedIn Salary** | General roles, decent for business tracks |
| **Blind** | Tech — anonymous, candid, real numbers |
| **Payscale** | Entry-level benchmarking |

Look at: base salary, bonus, equity (if any), and total comp — not just base.

---

## What's actually negotiable

**Always negotiable:**
- Base salary
- Signing bonus (often easier to move than base)
- Start date

**Sometimes negotiable:**
- Annual bonus target
- Equity / RSUs (at startups especially)
- Remote/hybrid flexibility
- Title

**Rarely negotiable at big companies for new grads:**
- Benefits structure
- Health insurance
- 401k match

At startups almost everything is negotiable. At big companies, base salary has bands — they can move within the band. Signing bonus is often where they have more flexibility.

---

## How to counter

Don't just say "can you do better." Give a number, give a reason.

**For base salary:**

> *"I'm really excited about this role and [Company]. Based on my research on Levels.fyi and conversations with people in similar roles, I was expecting something closer to [X]. Is there flexibility to get there?"*

Then stop talking. Let them fill the silence.

**If base won't move, ask about signing bonus:**

> *"I understand if the base is fixed within the band. Would there be flexibility on a signing bonus to bridge the gap?"*

**If you have a competing offer:**

> *"I want to be transparent — I have another offer at [X]. [Company] is my first choice, but I want to make sure I'm making the right decision. Is there any room to get closer to that number?"*

A competing offer is the strongest leverage you have. Use it if it's real. Never fabricate one.

---

## Evaluating the full offer

Salary is one line item. Evaluate the whole package.

```
Compensation
├── Base salary
├── Signing bonus (one-time)
├── Annual bonus (target % and how often it actually pays out)
└── Equity / RSUs (vesting schedule, cliff)

Benefits
├── Health insurance premium and quality
├── 401k match and vesting schedule
├── PTO (unlimited PTO is often less than a fixed number in practice)
└── Other: parental leave, FSA/HSA, wellness

Career
├── Learning and development budget
├── Promotion timeline
├── Who you'll be working for
└── Team size and growth trajectory

Logistics
├── Remote / hybrid / in-office
├── Start date
└── Relocation assistance if needed
```

A $5k higher salary at a company with poor mentorship and no growth path may be worth less than a $5k lower offer where you'll learn faster and get promoted sooner.

## For OPT candidates — additional considerations

Before accepting any offer:
- Confirm the company is enrolled in **E-Verify** (required for STEM OPT extension)
- Get clarity on their H1B sponsorship process and timeline
- Ask: "Has the company sponsored H1B before and what does that process look like?"

Get these answers before you sign. Not after.

---

## After you accept

- Get the offer letter in writing before giving notice anywhere
- Confirm start date in writing
- Do not post on LinkedIn until you've signed — offers are occasionally rescinded

---

## If the offer is rescinded

Rare but happens — especially in volatile markets.

- Get documentation that the offer was made and rescinded
- Talk to your university career center — they sometimes have relationships with companies and can advocate
- For OPT candidates: talk to your DSO immediately, the clock implications are real
- Some states have legal protections for rescinded offers
