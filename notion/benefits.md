[H1] Benefits — What They Are and What Actually Matters

[CALLOUT] 💡
Benefits can add $10,000–$30,000+ of real value to a compensation package. Most new grads look at base salary and ignore everything else. That's a mistake.

[DIVIDER]

[H2] Health Insurance (Medical)

In the US, health insurance is employer-sponsored. Your employer pays part of the premium — you pay the rest from each paycheck.

[H3] Key terms

[TABLE]
Term | What it means
Premium | What you pay per paycheck for coverage
Deductible | What you pay out-of-pocket before insurance kicks in (e.g. $1,500/year)
Copay | Fixed fee per visit (e.g. $20 per doctor visit)
Coinsurance | Your share after the deductible (e.g. 80/20 = insurance pays 80%, you pay 20%)
Out-of-pocket maximum | The most you'll pay in a year — after this, insurance covers 100%
In-network | Doctors with agreements with your insurer — always cheaper

[H3] Plan types

[TABLE]
Plan | What it means | Good for
PPO | See any doctor, no referral, higher premium | Flexibility, ongoing care
HMO | In-network only, referral needed for specialists, lower premium | Healthy, want lower cost
HDHP | High deductible, low premium — pairs with HSA | Healthy, young, want to save pre-tax

[CALLOUT] 💡 For most healthy new grads: HDHP + HSA is usually the best financial choice.

[TOGGLE] HSA (Health Savings Account) — what makes it powerful
Only available with HDHP plans.

[BULLET] Contribute pre-tax dollars
[BULLET] Money grows tax-free
[BULLET] Withdraw tax-free for medical expenses
[BULLET] Rolls over every year — it's yours forever, even if you leave
[BULLET] Triple tax advantage: the best savings vehicle available to you

Think of it as a second retirement account.

[TOGGLE] FSA (Flexible Spending Account)
[BULLET] Pre-tax dollars for medical expenses
[BULLET] Use it or lose it each year — unlike HSA
[BULLET] Good for predictable expenses (glasses, prescriptions, planned procedures)

What to ask: What's the monthly premium? What's the deductible? Does the company contribute to the HSA?

[DIVIDER]

[H2] Dental

Usually $10–20/month employee premium. Separate from medical.

[TABLE]
Care type | Typical coverage
Preventive (cleanings, X-rays) | 100% covered
Basic (fillings, extractions) | 70–80% covered
Major (crowns, root canals) | 50% covered
Orthodontics (braces, Invisalign) | Separate lifetime max (~$1,500–$2,000)

Most plans cap annual benefits at $1,000–$2,000. If you need major work, you pay the rest.

[DIVIDER]

[H2] Vision

Usually $5–10/month. Covers annual eye exam + allowance for glasses or contacts ($150–$300/year). If you wear glasses or contacts, this pays for itself immediately.

[DIVIDER]

[H2] Life Insurance

Employer typically provides 1–2x your annual salary free. Additional coverage available through payroll deductions.

[CALLOUT] 💡 If you're single with no dependents, the free employer coverage is enough. Life insurance matters more when others depend on your income.

[DIVIDER]

[H2] 401k — Retirement

Tax-advantaged retirement account. You contribute a % of your paycheck — pre-tax (Traditional) or post-tax (Roth).

[CALLOUT] ⚠️ The employer match is free money. Always contribute at least enough to get the full match.

Example: $80,000 salary, employer matches 100% up to 4%
→ You contribute $3,200 → employer adds $3,200 free
→ That's an instant 100% return on your contribution

[TOGGLE] Vesting schedule — the catch
The employer match often vests over time. You only "own" it after staying a certain amount of time.

[TABLE]
Type | What it means
Cliff vesting | 100% vested after X years — get nothing if you leave before
Graded vesting | % per year — keep what's vested if you leave early
Immediate | You own the match right away — best case

[TOGGLE] Traditional vs Roth 401k
[TABLE]
Type | How it works | Good for
Traditional | Pre-tax now, pay taxes at withdrawal | Lower tax bracket in retirement
Roth | Post-tax now, tax-free at withdrawal | New grads — you're likely in a lower bracket now than later

What to ask: What's the match % and up to what salary %? What's the vesting schedule?

[DIVIDER]

[H2] Equity

[TOGGLE] RSUs (Restricted Stock Units) — most common at public companies
Company grants you shares that vest over time. When they vest, you own them and can sell.

Typical schedule: 4-year vest, 1-year cliff
[BULLET] Nothing vests until month 12
[BULLET] 25% vests at end of year 1
[BULLET] Remaining 75% vests monthly or quarterly over 3 more years

Tax: RSUs are taxed as ordinary income when they vest. Company withholds shares automatically.

Key question: Is this a public company (can sell immediately) or private (illiquid until IPO/acquisition)?

[TOGGLE] Stock Options — more common at startups
Right to buy company shares at a fixed strike price. Value only if the company grows above that price.

[TABLE]
Type | Tax treatment
ISO (Incentive Stock Options) | Better tax treatment, for employees
NSO (Non-qualified Stock Options) | Less favorable tax treatment

For new grads at startups: treat options as a lottery ticket, not a salary component. They can be life-changing or worthless.

What to ask: How many options, what's the strike price, what's the 409A valuation, what's the vesting schedule?

[TOGGLE] ESOP (Employee Stock Ownership Plan)
Less common in tech. Company contributes shares to a retirement-like plan on your behalf. Treat it like a pension — long-term, not liquid.

[TOGGLE] ESPP (Employee Stock Purchase Plan) — public companies only
Buy company stock at a discount (usually 10–15%) through payroll deductions.

[CALLOUT] 💡 ESPP is almost always worth participating in — even a straight 15% discount is a guaranteed return if you sell immediately. Many plans also have a lookback provision that can increase the effective discount significantly.

What to ask: What's the discount rate? Is there a lookback provision?

[DIVIDER]

[H2] Other benefits worth evaluating

[TABLE]
Benefit | What to know
PTO | Fixed PTO (e.g. 15 days) is often better than unlimited in practice — ask what people actually take
Commuter benefits | Pre-tax transit/parking — saves 20–30% on commute costs
Remote work stipend | One-time home office setup ($500–$2,000) + monthly internet stipend
L&D budget | $1,000–$5,000/year for courses, conferences, certifications
Wellness / gym stipend | $50–$100/month toward gym or fitness apps
Mental health / EAP | Therapy sessions, mental health apps — varies widely
Parental leave | Federal minimum is 12 weeks unpaid — companies vary from 0 to 4+ months paid

[H2] Questions to ask before accepting

[TOGGLE] Health insurance
☐ What's the monthly premium for individual coverage?
☐ What plan types are available (PPO, HMO, HDHP)?
☐ Does the company contribute to the HSA?

[TOGGLE] 401k
☐ What's the employer match and up to what % of salary?
☐ What's the vesting schedule on the match?
☐ Is Roth 401k available?

[TOGGLE] Equity
☐ What's the total RSU / option grant?
☐ What's the vesting schedule and cliff?
☐ For options: what's the strike price and current 409A valuation?
☐ Is ESPP available? What's the discount and lookback?

[TOGGLE] Other
☐ How many PTO days? What do people actually take?
☐ Is there a remote work or home office stipend?
☐ What's the L&D budget?

[DIVIDER]

[H2] For OPT / Visa candidates

Most benefits are available to F-1 OPT employees the same as any other employee — but confirm with HR. Some companies have waiting periods (30–60 days).

If you're considering leaving a job on OPT: understand what happens to unvested equity and your 401k match vesting before making any decisions.
