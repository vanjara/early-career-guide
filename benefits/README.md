# Benefits — What They Are and What Actually Matters

## Why this section exists

Most new grads look at one number — base salary — and ignore everything else. That's a mistake.

Benefits can add $10,000–$30,000+ of real value to a compensation package. They can also be confusing, especially if you're coming from outside the US where employer-sponsored benefits work differently or don't exist at all.

This section explains what each benefit is, what to look for, and what questions to ask before you accept an offer.

---

## Health Insurance (Medical)

In the US, health insurance is mostly employer-sponsored. Your employer pays a portion of the premium — you pay the rest, deducted from each paycheck.

**Key terms:**

```
Premium        What you pay per paycheck for coverage

Deductible     What you pay out-of-pocket before insurance kicks in
               e.g. $1,500 deductible = you pay the first $1,500 of
               medical costs each year yourself

Copay          Fixed amount you pay per visit (e.g. $20 for a doctor visit)

Coinsurance    Your share after the deductible
               e.g. 80/20 = insurance pays 80%, you pay 20%

Out-of-pocket  The most you'll pay in a year — after this,
maximum        insurance covers 100%

In-network     Doctors/hospitals with agreements with your insurer
               Always cheaper than out-of-network
```

**Plan types:**

| Plan | What it means | Good for |
|---|---|---|
| **PPO** | See any doctor, no referral needed, higher premium | Flexibility, ongoing care |
| **HMO** | Must use in-network, need referral for specialists, lower premium | Healthy, want lower cost |
| **HDHP** | High deductible, low premium — pairs with HSA | Healthy, young, want to save pre-tax |

**For most healthy new grads:** An HDHP paired with an HSA is usually the best financial choice.

**HSA (Health Savings Account):**
- Only available with HDHP plans
- Contribute pre-tax dollars, invest them, withdraw tax-free for medical expenses
- Money rolls over every year — it's yours forever, even if you leave the company
- Triple tax advantage: pre-tax contributions + tax-free growth + tax-free withdrawals
- Think of it as a second retirement account

**FSA (Flexible Spending Account):**
- Pre-tax dollars for medical expenses
- Use it or lose it each year — unlike HSA
- Good for predictable expenses (glasses, prescriptions, planned procedures)

**What to ask:** What's the monthly premium for individual coverage? What's the deductible? Does the company contribute to the HSA?

## Dental

Usually separate from medical. Typically $10-20/month employee premium.

**How coverage typically works:**

```
Preventive care     (cleanings, X-rays)        100% covered
Basic care          (fillings, extractions)     70–80% covered
Major care          (crowns, root canals)       50% covered
Orthodontics        (braces, Invisalign)        separate lifetime max (~$1,500–$2,000)
```

Most dental plans cap annual benefits at $1,000–$2,000. If you need major work, you pay the rest.

**What to ask:** What's the annual maximum? Is orthodontics covered?

---

## Vision

Usually $5-10/month. Covers:
- Annual eye exam (fully covered or small copay)
- Allowance for glasses or contacts — usually $150-$300/year
- Discounts on LASIK

If you wear glasses or contacts, this pays for itself immediately.

---

## Life Insurance

Employer typically provides basic life insurance free — usually 1x or 2x your annual salary. Additional coverage can be purchased through payroll deductions.

**Honest take for most new grads:** If you're single with no dependents, the free employer coverage is enough. Life insurance matters more when others depend on your income.

## 401k — Retirement

A tax-advantaged retirement account. You contribute a percentage of your paycheck — pre-tax (Traditional) or post-tax (Roth).

**The employer match — never leave this on the table:**

> *"We match 100% of contributions up to 4% of your salary"*

This means: if you earn $80,000 and contribute 4% ($3,200), the company adds $3,200 free. That's the best return on investment you'll ever get.

Always contribute at least enough to get the full match.

**Vesting schedule — the catch:**

The employer match often has a vesting schedule — you only "own" it after staying a certain amount of time.

```
Cliff vesting     100% vested after 2 years — get nothing if you leave before
Graded vesting    25% per year — keep what's vested if you leave early
Immediate         You own the match right away — best case
```

**Traditional vs Roth 401k:**

```
Traditional    Pre-tax contributions → pay taxes at withdrawal in retirement
               Good if you expect lower tax bracket in retirement

Roth           Post-tax contributions → withdraw tax-free in retirement
               Good for new grads — you're likely in a lower bracket now
               than you will be later
```

**What to ask:** What's the match % and up to what salary %? What's the vesting schedule?

## Equity

### RSUs (Restricted Stock Units) — most common at public companies

The company grants you shares that vest over time. When they vest, you own them and can sell.

```
Typical schedule:   4-year vest, 1-year cliff
1-year cliff:       Nothing vests until month 12, then 25% vests at once
After cliff:        Remaining 75% vests monthly or quarterly over 3 more years
```

**Tax:** RSUs are taxed as ordinary income when they vest. Your company withholds shares to cover taxes automatically.

**Key question:** Is this a public company (can sell immediately) or private (illiquid until IPO or acquisition)?

---

### Stock Options — more common at startups

The right to **buy** company shares at a fixed price (strike price).

Value only if the company grows: if strike price is $10 and shares are worth $25, your option is worth $15. If the company never grows above $10, your options are worth nothing.

```
ISO (Incentive Stock Options)     Better tax treatment, for employees
NSO (Non-qualified Stock Options) Less favorable tax treatment
```

**For new grads at startups:** Options can be life-changing or worthless. Don't count on them for financial planning. Treat them as a lottery ticket, not a salary component.

**What to ask:** How many options, what's the strike price, what's the current 409A valuation, what's the vesting schedule?

---

### ESOP (Employee Stock Ownership Plan)

Less common in tech. The company contributes shares into a retirement-like plan on your behalf over time. If you encounter one, treat it like a pension — long-term value, not liquid.

---

### ESPP (Employee Stock Purchase Plan) — public companies only

Lets you buy company stock at a discount (usually 10–15%) through payroll deductions.

**Why it's almost always worth participating:**

Even a straight 15% discount = guaranteed 15% return if you sell immediately. Many plans also have a lookback provision — shares are purchased at 15% off the lower of the price at the start or end of the offering period, which can dramatically increase the effective return.

**What to ask:** What's the discount rate? Is there a lookback provision? What's the contribution limit?

## Other Benefits Worth Evaluating

**PTO (Paid Time Off)**

Fixed PTO (e.g., 15 days) is often better than unlimited PTO in practice. People take less time with unlimited because there's no clear entitlement. Ask what the average employee actually takes.

Also check: does unused PTO pay out when you leave? In some states (California) it must by law.

**Commuter Benefits**

Pre-tax dollars for public transit or parking. Saves 20-30% on commute costs depending on your tax bracket.

**Remote Work Stipend**

One-time home office setup allowance ($500-$2,000) + monthly internet stipend. Increasingly common.

**Learning & Development Budget**

$1,000-$5,000/year for courses, conferences, certifications. Most valuable early career when skill-building compounds most.

**Wellness / Gym Stipend**

$50-$100/month toward gym or fitness apps.

**Mental Health**

Check what the EAP (Employee Assistance Program) covers — therapy sessions, mental health apps, counseling.

**Parental Leave**

US federal law only mandates 12 weeks unpaid. Companies vary widely — some offer 4+ months paid, others offer nothing beyond the minimum.

## How to Compare Two Offers on Benefits

Don't just compare salaries. Build a simple comparison:

```
                          Offer A     Offer B
Base salary               $85,000     $80,000
Annual bonus (target)      $5,000      $8,000
RSUs (annualized)         $10,000     $15,000
401k match                 $2,400      $3,200
ESPP discount                  —       $2,000 est.
Health premium (annual)   -$1,200        -$600

Total comp estimate      $101,200    $107,600
```

The $5,000 lower base may actually be worth more in total comp.

## Questions to Ask Before Accepting

```
Health
□ What's the monthly premium for individual coverage?
□ What plan types are available?
□ Does the company contribute to the HSA?

401k
□ What's the employer match and up to what %?
□ What's the vesting schedule on the match?
□ Is Roth 401k available?

Equity
□ What's the total RSU / option grant?
□ What's the vesting schedule?
□ For options: what's the strike price and current 409A valuation?
□ Is ESPP available? What's the discount and lookback?

Other
□ How many PTO days? What do people actually take?
□ Is there a remote work / home office stipend?
□ What's the L&D budget?
```

## For OPT / Visa Candidates

Most benefits are available to F-1 OPT employees the same as any other employee. But confirm with HR — some companies have waiting periods (benefits start after 30 or 60 days).

If you leave a job on OPT: understand what happens to unvested equity and your 401k match vesting before making any decisions.
