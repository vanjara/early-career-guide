# Why Easy Apply Doesn't Work (And What to Do Instead)

> 🤖 Easy Apply has become less effective as application volume has increased. See the AI Age section.

> ℹ️ This guide is maintained by volunteers and reflects our best understanding of the job market. Company hiring practices, visa rules, and market conditions change. Cross-check anything critical — visa rules, hiring timelines, salary ranges — against primary sources before acting. Last updated: March 2026.

> ⚠️ Easy Apply is optimized for your convenience, not your chances. It's designed to make applying feel productive. It isn't.

---

## What's actually happening when you click Easy Apply

When a company posts on LinkedIn, they get 200–500 Easy Apply submissions in the first 48 hours. A recruiter is not reading 400 resumes. They're scanning 20, maybe 30. The rest get archived.

> 💡 Job boards are where candidates go. Referrals are where jobs get filled. Studies consistently show 70–80% of jobs are filled through networking.

---

## What to do instead

Use all three approaches in parallel.

---

### 1. Targeted direct applications

Where possible, apply directly on the company’s career page rather than through Easy Apply.

Why this works:

- Signals genuine interest in that specific company
- Often surfaces differently in recruiting systems than third-party aggregator submissions
- Forces you to be selective, which means better-matched applications
How to do it:

1. Build a list of 20–30 target companies you actually want to work at
1. Bookmark their careers pages
1. Apply directly, tailoring your resume to each role
1. Track every application — company, role, date, status, next step
> 💡 Aim for 10–15 quality applications per week, not 50 generic ones.

---

### 2. Referrals — highest conversion, most effort

A referral from someone who knows your work meaningfully improves your chances of being seen. A referral from someone who barely knows you carries little weight — hiring managers can tell the difference. The value is in the relationship, not the act of being referred.

1. Find a role you want to apply to
1. Search LinkedIn for people at that company — filter by university, city, mutual connections
1. Send a short, specific message — not 'can you refer me'
1. Have a conversation first — ask about their experience, the team, the role
1. If it goes well, they'll offer or you can ask directly
### ✅ Message that works

> "Hi [Name], I noticed you work at [Company] on the [Team] team. I'm a recent grad in [field] and I'm really interested in the [Role] — I came across your profile because we both went to [University]. Would you have 15 minutes to chat about your experience there? No pressure at all."

### ❌ Message that doesn't work

> "Hi, I am interested in the [Role] position at your company. Can you refer me?"

People refer candidates they've talked to, not strangers who ask cold.

A referral doesn’t guarantee an interview. It increases the chance a human actually reads your resume.

If someone doesn’t respond to your outreach, follow up once after 5–7 days. Then move on.

---

### 3. Warm outreach to hiring managers

For smaller companies and startups, reach the hiring manager directly — not HR.

How to find them: LinkedIn search '[Company] + [Engineering Manager / Data Manager / Finance Lead]'

### ✅ Message template

> "Hi [Name], I wanted to follow up on my application for [Role]. My background in [specific area] is directly relevant — [one concrete sentence: built X, led Y, delivered Z]. I am happy to answer any questions or provide additional context."

---

## Your weekly system

Bursts create frustration. Pipelines create interviews.

---

## The mindset shift

> 💡 Stop thinking of job searching as submitting applications. Start thinking of it as having conversations. Applications are a byproduct of conversations.
