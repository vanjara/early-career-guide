# Job Search Strategy
## Why Easy Apply Doesn't Work (And What to Do Instead)

## What's actually happening when you click Easy Apply

When a company posts a job on LinkedIn, they might get **200-500 Easy Apply submissions in the first 48 hours.** For a popular role at a recognizable company, that number is higher.

A recruiter at that company is not reading 400 resumes. They're scanning 20, maybe 30. The rest get archived.

How do they pick the 30? Often it's whoever applied first, whoever came referred, or whoever the ATS scored highest. Your carefully written resume is competing with 400 others and the selection is largely arbitrary.

Easy Apply is optimized for **your convenience**, not your chances. It's designed to make applying feel productive. It isn't.

---

## The deeper problem

Easy Apply trains you into the wrong behavior:

- Apply to 50 jobs → hear back from 2 → conclude the market is tough
- Apply to 50 more → same result
- Repeat until demoralized

The problem isn't the market. It's the channel.

**Job boards are where candidates go. Referrals are where jobs get filled.**

---

## What to do instead

Three approaches that actually work. Use all three in parallel.

---

### 1. Targeted direct applications (your baseline)

Where possible, apply directly on the company's career page rather than through Easy Apply.

Why this is better:
- Signals genuine interest in the company specifically
- Often surfaces differently in recruiting systems than third-party aggregator submissions
- Forces you to be more selective, which means better-matched applications

How to do it:
- Build a list of 20-30 target companies you actually want to work at
- Bookmark their careers pages
- Apply directly there, tailoring your resume to each role
- Track every application (a simple spreadsheet: company, role, date applied, status, next step)

Aim for **10-15 quality applications per week**, not 50 generic ones.

---

### 2. Referrals (highest conversion, most effort)

A referral from someone who knows your work meaningfully improves your chances of being seen. A referral from someone who barely knows you carries little weight — hiring managers can tell the difference. The value is in the relationship, not the act of being referred.

The actual process:

1. Find a role you want to apply to
2. Search LinkedIn for people who work at that company — filter by your university, your city, mutual connections
3. Send a short, specific message (not "can you refer me")
4. Have a conversation first — ask about their experience, the team, the role
5. If it goes well, they'll offer or you can ask directly

**The message that works:**

> *"Hi [Name], I noticed you work at [Company] on the [Team] team. I'm a recent grad in [field] and I'm really interested in the [Role] — I came across your profile because we both went to [University / mutual connection]. Would you have 15 minutes to chat about your experience there? No pressure at all."*

**What doesn't work:**
> *"Hi, I am interested in the [Role] position at your company. Can you refer me?"*

People refer candidates they've talked to, not strangers who ask cold.

A referral doesn't guarantee an interview. It increases the chance a human actually reads your resume.

If someone doesn't respond to your outreach, follow up once after 5–7 days. Then move on.

---

### 3. Outreach to hiring managers

For smaller companies or startups, you can sometimes reach the hiring manager directly. For larger companies, the recruiter listed on the posting is the right contact — HMs at large companies typically don't manage inbound candidate outreach.

How to find them: LinkedIn search for "[Company] + [Engineering Manager / Data Manager / Finance Lead]"

**The message:**

> *"Hi [Name], I wanted to follow up on my application for [Role]. My background in [specific area] is directly relevant — [one concrete sentence: built X, led Y, delivered Z]. I am happy to answer any questions or provide additional context."*

Keep it brief, specific, and professional. Lead with what's relevant — don't make them search for the connection.

---

## The actual system

```
Weekly job search routine:

Monday
└── Research 5 target companies
└── Find 1-2 people at each to reach out to

Tuesday - Thursday
└── Send 5-10 personalized LinkedIn messages
└── Apply directly on 5-10 company career pages (tailored resume)

Friday
└── Follow up on applications from 2 weeks ago
└── Update your tracking spreadsheet
└── Respond to any recruiter messages
```

This is slower than clicking Easy Apply 50 times. It converts at a higher rate.

Bursts create frustration. Pipelines create interviews.

---

## One mindset shift

Stop thinking of job searching as **submitting applications**.

Start thinking of it as **having conversations**.

Applications are a byproduct of conversations. The more people you talk to — recruiters, alumni, hiring managers, people in roles you want — the faster you move. Every conversation either leads somewhere or teaches you something.
