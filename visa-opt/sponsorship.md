# Finding Companies That Actually Sponsor

## The problem with job postings

"Visa sponsorship available" in a job posting means very little. It's often copy-pasted from a template, added by an overeager recruiter, or refers to TN/E-3 visas (not H1B).

The only thing that matters is whether a company has actually filed H1B petitions before — and you can look this up.

---

## How to vet a company — step by step

**H1Bdata.info**
1. Go to h1bdata.info
2. Search by employer name
3. Look at:
   - Number of petitions filed (last 3 years)
   - Approval vs. denial rate
   - Job titles they've sponsored (is your role in there?)
   - Salary ranges on sponsored roles

**Department of Labor disclosure data**
- dol.gov has raw H1B disclosure data
- More comprehensive but harder to navigate than H1Bdata.info
- Use this to cross-check if you want more detail

**myvisajobs.com**
- Search by employer or job title
- Shows historical sponsorship with salary ranges
- Good for finding companies you haven't thought of

---

## What the data tells you

| Signal | What it means |
|---|---|
| 100+ petitions, 90%+ approval rate | Established sponsor, real process |
| 10-50 petitions, good approval rate | Sponsors selectively — ask about process early |
| 1-5 petitions total | Rare, case-by-case — high risk |
| Zero petitions | Almost certainly won't sponsor despite what they say |
| High denial rate | May have compliance issues — ask questions |

---

## General patterns

These are patterns, not guarantees. Always verify individually.

**Typically sponsor consistently:**
- Large tech companies (FAANG, Microsoft, Salesforce, etc.)
- Big 4 consulting (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG) — for specific roles
- Major banks and financial institutions — for tech and some analyst roles
- Large healthcare and pharma companies

**Varies widely:**
- Startups — check H1Bdata.info, ask directly in recruiter screen
- Mid-size tech companies — some do, some don't
- Consulting boutiques — depends heavily on the firm

**Rarely or never sponsor:**
- Government contractors (often can't due to security clearance requirements)
- Small businesses and local companies
- Nonprofits (some do, many can't afford the legal fees)
- Retail and hospitality companies

---

## Questions to ask the recruiter

Ask these early — recruiter screen or right after:

> *"Does [Company] sponsor H1B visas for this role?"*

> *"Has the company sponsored H1B visas for [your role type] before?"*

> *"Is the company enrolled in E-Verify?"* (required for STEM OPT extension)

> *"What does your H1B sponsorship process look like — do you work with outside counsel?"*

A company with a real process will answer these confidently. Vague answers ("we'll cross that bridge") are a red flag.

---

## Getting it in writing

If H1B sponsorship is important to your decision to accept, ask for it to be noted in your offer letter or in a follow-up email.

Something simple:
> *"Thank you for the offer. Just to confirm our conversation — [Company] will support my H1B petition filing in April [year]. Can you confirm this in writing?"*

Most companies won't put it in the offer letter formally, but getting a written email confirmation from HR is reasonable and worth asking for.
