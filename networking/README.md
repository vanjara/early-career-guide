# Networking

## Reframe it first

Most people hear "networking" and picture awkward events, forced small talk, and handing out business cards. That's not what this is.

Networking for job search is simple: **Talk to people doing the work you want to do. Learn from them.**

You're not asking for favors. You're not being transactional. You're having conversations with people who were in your exact position not long ago — and most of them are happy to help because someone helped them.

The candidates who get jobs fastest are not always the most qualified. They're the ones who are **known by someone inside the company.**

---

## The three sources of people to talk to

```
1. Alumni from your university       — easiest to reach, highest response rate
2. People you've worked with before  — internships, part-time jobs, class projects
3. Cold outreach to strangers        — harder, but scales
```

Start with 1 and 2. Don't start with cold outreach.

---

## Alumni network — step by step

This is the most underused resource for new grads. Alumni respond because you share something real — the same school, professors, probably similar experiences.

**How to find them on LinkedIn:**
1. Go to your university's LinkedIn page
2. Click "Alumni"
3. Filter by: Where they work, What they do, When they graduated
4. Look for people 2-5 years ahead of you — recent enough to remember the struggle, established enough to have influence

**The message:**

> *"Hi [Name], I'm a recent [University] grad in [field] and came across your profile. I'm exploring roles in [area] and noticed you've been at [Company] for a couple of years — I'd love to hear how you got there and what the work is actually like. Would you have 20 minutes for a quick call sometime this week or next?"*

What makes this work:
- You're not asking for a job or a referral
- You have a specific reason for reaching out (same school, their specific role)
- The ask is small (20 minutes)

**Response rate reality:** Expect 20-30% response rate on good outreach. Don't take non-responses personally. If 10 people don't respond, 2-3 will. That's 2-3 conversations you wouldn't have had.

## The informational interview

A 20-30 minute call where you ask someone about their experience. Not a job interview — a conversation. Most calls last 15–20 minutes. Respect that.

**What to ask:**

```
Opening
- "How did you end up at [Company] / in [field]?"
- "What does a typical week look like for you?"

The real stuff
- "What do you wish you'd known when you were job searching?"
- "What do companies in this space actually look for in new grads?"
- "Is there anyone else you'd recommend I talk to?"

The close
- "Is there anything I could send you — resume, portfolio — where you could give me feedback?"
```

Always ask the "anyone else" question. Getting introduced to one more person compounds quickly. Five conversations can turn into fifteen.

**After the call:**

Send a thank you within 24 hours. Two sentences is fine:

> *"Thanks so much for your time today — the advice on [specific thing they said] was really helpful. I'll keep you posted on how the search goes."*

Then actually keep them posted when something happens.

## Why someone refers you

A referral only works if the person referring you is willing to put their name next to yours. When someone refers a candidate, they're taking a reputational risk — if you bomb the interview or turn out to be a bad fit, it reflects on them.

People refer you when:
- They've seen your work — through a project, a conversation, something you built or wrote
- The interaction left them thinking "this person gets it"
- They trust you enough to attach their name to you

This is why asking cold for a referral doesn't work. You're asking someone to take a reputational risk for a stranger. The conversation has to come first — the referral is what happens when it goes well.

A referral from someone who barely knows you also carries little weight. Hiring managers can tell the difference between "I worked with this person and they're excellent" and "someone I chatted with once asked me to submit their name." The value is in the relationship, not the act of being referred.

## Cold outreach on LinkedIn

Once you've exhausted alumni and warm connections, go broader.

**Hooks that work:**
- You read something they wrote (LinkedIn post, article)
- You're applying to their company and want to learn more before applying
- You share a specific background (same city, same undergrad major, similar career path)

**Template:**

> *"Hi [Name], I've been following your posts on [topic] and found your take on [specific thing] really useful. I'm a recent grad exploring roles in [area] and your path from [X to Y] caught my attention. Would you be open to a 20-minute call? Happy to work around your schedule."*

**What kills cold outreach:**
- Generic openers ("I am very passionate about your company")
- Attaching your resume in the first message
- Asking for a referral before having a conversation
- Copy-pasting the same message to 50 people — people can tell

**On hiring managers:** Reaching out to a hiring manager directly works best at smaller companies and startups. At larger companies, everything routes through recruiters — contact the recruiter listed on the posting instead.

## LinkedIn profile — the other side of networking

When people receive your outreach, they look at your profile. It needs to hold up.

**Headline** — don't just put your degree or "seeking opportunities"

Bad: *"Computer Science Graduate | Looking for Full-Time Opportunities"*

Good: *"Data Analyst | Python, SQL, Tableau | Recent grad @ [University]"*

**About section** — 3-4 sentences, written like a human not a cover letter

> *"I'm a recent Business Analytics grad from [University] with a focus on financial modeling and data visualization. I've worked on [X] and [Y] and I'm now looking for analyst roles where I can work with messy data and turn it into decisions. Always open to conversations about data, finance, or [relevant interest]."*

**Experience** — same rules as resume. Action verbs, results, numbers.

## Building a simple system

A network is built through follow-ups, not introductions. One conversation and disappearing doesn't compound. Staying in touch does.

```
Weekly networking routine:

- 5 new outreach messages sent
- 1-2 calls or conversations
- Follow up on anyone you spoke to 1-2 weeks ago
- Connect with anyone you met or spoke to (same day)
```

Track it in the same spreadsheet as your applications:

```
Name | Company | How connected | Date reached | Status | Notes
```
